# The sea loan is a contingent claim not a loan

The sea loan (bottomry, respondentia; Roman *foenus nauticum*) is a loan in name only. A loan in the strict sense — *mutuum* — is an unconditional obligation to return principal. The sea loan's defining feature is that repayment is *conditional*: if the vessel is lost, the borrower owes nothing. On the defining feature of a loan, it fails. What remains, once you remove unconditional repayment, is a *contingent claim* — an underwriting contract with a capital advance stapled to it. Roman law already saw the hybridity: the excess over principal was *pretium periculi*, the price of risk borne, not *usura* on a *mutuum*, and was therefore exempt from the ordinary interest ceiling.

The correct modern analogue is a **contingent claim / embedded option**, not a future. The borrower holds a *put* on the venture (he walks away on sea-peril); the lender has *written* that put and holds a risky, default-prone claim priced by the *pretium periculi*. This distinction is load-bearing for the Islamic argument: Islam *permitted* the pure forward (*salam*), so futurity as such was never the problem — the *optionality and aleatory payoff* are.

So "loan" is the **fossil name** of a bundled instrument: proto-insurance wearing a loan's clothing, adopted because a world with no autonomous category for priced risk had only the loan as a licit wrapper. Its whole subsequent history is the unbundling of its two functions. Specify the contingency: the sea loan is contingent on *physical arrival* (proto-*insurance*), not *commercial outcome* (proto-*equity*).
