# Whiggish history of finance names the ascent narrative

The phrase descends from Herbert Butterfield, *The Whig Interpretation of History* (1931). Butterfield's target was a specific historiographical vice: reading the past as an inevitable ascent toward the present, organized around the ratification of present values. Its four markers: **teleology** (the present as culmination, not contingency), **present-centeredness** (the past ransacked for anticipations of what we now approve), **winners' bias** (whoever prevailed cast as "progressive," their opponents as reactionary obstacles), and the **flattening of contingency** into inevitability.

Transposed to finance, a "Whiggish history of finance" is the standard ascent narrative: barter → coinage → deposit banking → joint-stock → central banking → securitization and derivatives, each stage more complete, more efficient, better at pricing and distributing risk than the last. Ferguson's *The Ascent of Money* wears the teleology in its title; Goetzmann's *Money Changes Everything* is a subtler version. Episodes of ruin become mere friction on the upward curve — growing pains rather than structural features.

Note that this is an *extension by analogy*: Butterfield's thesis was about constitutional and political history (and he was arguably Whiggish himself in *The Origins of Modern Science*). Say so, because someone will.
