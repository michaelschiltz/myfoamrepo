# Decoupling substitutes proxy legibility for outcome legibility

Attenuating outcome-coupling does not abolish evaluation. It forces the growth of a surrogate organ.

Organizations must still allocate — promote, fund, price, partner. Where the true signal (a realized outcome borne by the decider) is unavailable or arrives too late, allocation runs on proxies: credentials, prestige, rankings, ratings, brand, "track record" as narrative. Goodhart's law then supplies the decay, since a surrogate ceases to measure once it is targeted.

The consequence is sharper than lost information. **Selection does not stop; it retargets.** Selection inside the decoupled organization is ferocious, but it runs on proxy-performance rather than outcome-performance and therefore breeds skill at proxy-optimization — competence at *appearing* competent. Misdirected selection is worse than weak selection: weak selection leaves error undisturbed, whereas directional selection actively cultivates the wrong capacity.

The historical spine matters, because it stops this being a presentist complaint. The chartered companies invented surrogate monitoring precisely because distance and delay had destroyed outcome-legibility: a factor's performance could not be observed for six to twelve months, so the companies ran on correspondence, ledgers, sureties and bonds, oaths, and the placement of kin. Harris devotes chapters ten and eleven to the problem of the remote employee [verify the framing]. **The same distance that made capital lock-in necessary also made outcome-legibility impossible and forced the growth of substitute signals.** Modern ratings, rankings, and credentialism are lineal descendants of the VOC's monitoring problem, not a late-capitalist novelty.

This is the analytically serious core of Taleb's polemic against the credentialed expert, stated without the polemic — and it is available only from both halves of the framework at once, which is why he cannot reach it.
