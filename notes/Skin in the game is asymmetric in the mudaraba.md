# Skin in the game is asymmetric in the mudaraba

Do not write that "both partners keep skin in the game" without qualification. In the *mudaraba* the monetary loss falls on the *rabb al-māl* (capital provider) **alone**. The *mudārib* (agent) holds the capital as *amāna* (trust) and is liable only where he was negligent or exceeded his instructions — *taʿaddī*, *taqṣīr*, *mukhālafa* — at which point *ḍamān* (liability) attaches. He risks labour, time, and reputation; he does not risk capital.

The modern analogue is exact and worth using: an LP/GP fund in which the general partner contributes no capital and takes carried interest. The profit share is a residual claim determined ex post, not a wage fixed ex ante — which is Harris's point, and correct — but it is a residual claim on the **upside only**.

This is the classical principal-agent hazard, and the tradition recognized it. The elaborate negligence-and-instructions liability doctrine exists precisely to restore the agent's skin in the game where the profit share alone left it thin. That strengthens the argument rather than weakening it: it shows the fiqh reasoning about trajectory-binding *explicitly*, as a designed correction, rather than stumbling into it.

Note the contrast with the sea loan, where the asymmetry runs the other way: there the lender bears the peril tail and the borrower walks away on loss, which is what makes fraud pay. See [[Scuttling rewards fraud in the sea loan]].
