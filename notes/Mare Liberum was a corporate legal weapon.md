# Mare Liberum was a corporate legal weapon

*Mare Liberum* (1609) was not a neutral principle. It was a VOC brief, extracted from *De Jure Praedae*, written to justify the Dutch seizure of the *Santa Catarina* and to break the Portuguese *mare clausum*. Freedom of the seas *originates as a corporate legal weapon*. And the moment the Dutch held the Spice Islands, they asserted their own monopoly by contract and pass against the English and against Asian producers — *mare clausum* in all but name. Selden answered for England (1635); Freitas for Portugal (*De Justo Imperio*, ~1625 — **verify date**).

Freedom and its denial are thus two settings of a single legal-violence machine, switched according to whether you are breaking someone's monopoly or building your own. This dissolves the very category — "trade enterprise" — on which Harris's bracketing depends. The company does not trade *under* law and turn violent *later*; it manufactures the legal categories — prize, pass, monopoly, charter — that make its coercion licit from the first voyage. The **charter** is the two-sided instrument: asset partitioning and lock-in on the inside, the exclusion of everyone else from a trade declared yours on the outside.

Theoretical anchors: Benjamin's *rechtsetzende* / *rechtserhaltende Gewalt* — *Mare Liberum* as law-making violence that shatters the Iberian order; the cartaz and the monopoly as law-preserving violence — and Robert Cover, "Violence and the Word" (law as a thing done "upon a field of pain and death").
