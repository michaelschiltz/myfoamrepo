# Islamic doctrine refuses risk-commodification at step one

The Islamic objection to the sea loan is over-determined, but its *binding* constraint is not *riba*. Jurists were virtuosi at neutralizing *riba* — *bay' al-'ina*, the double sale, the whole *hiyal* apparatus. Had *riba* been the binding constraint, a sea-loan workaround was trivially within reach and would have appeared. Its absence tells you *riba* was not binding.

Two things were. **Gharar** (contractual uncertainty) shading into **maysir** (gambling) — the aleatory core, a payoff hinging on a genuinely uncertain binary event (arrival vs wreck) — is far harder to launder, because the wager *is* the contract; strip it out and the instrument is destroyed. And the *qirad* already held the niche (see [[The qirad envelops the sea loan]]). Note that futurity *per se* was licit: **salam**, prepayment now for specified fungibles delivered at a set future date, was a permitted forward. So doctrine did not refuse to price the future; it refused to let a party *sell pure uncertainty*.

Read this as the law's **revealed preference**: a gravitational bias toward ergodicity-preserving, skin-in-the-game instruments and against the first move toward the veil. Taleb says nearly this in praise of Mediterranean PLS finance; it can be made rigorous where he leaves it aphoristic.

Two cautions so a referee cannot knock it over. State it as the law's *revealed preference*, not as practice on the water — *hiyal* blurred the line constantly, and effective risk-pricing crept back via *mudaraba* ratios and *murabaha* markups. And test whether the claim should be **non-institutionalization** rather than **absence**: the Geniza evidence (Goitein, *Mediterranean Society*; and now perhaps the Afghan Geniza) suggests maritime lending was not wholly unknown.
