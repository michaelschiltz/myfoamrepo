# The mudaraba is revocable not locked in

Classical fiqh predominantly classifies the *mudaraba*/*qirad* as an *ʿaqd jāʾiz* — a non-binding, revocable contract — rather than an *ʿaqd lāzim*. Either party may in principle rescind; the contract terminates on the death or incapacity of either party; and it dissolves at the venture's conclusion rather than persisting. Maliki jurists restrict revocation once the agent has commenced work [verify against fiqh sources — the schools diverge here].

This kills a tempting but false formulation: that Islamic doctrine requires both partners to see the venture through to the end and forbids any uncoupling. It does not. The *qirad* is among the *most* easily dissolved commercial forms in the pre-modern world. What doctrine binds is **payoff to outcome**, not **partner to duration**. See [[Lock-in and outcome-coupling are distinct bindings]].

The correction is load-bearing rather than pedantic. Harris's entire corporation thesis turns on lock-in being precisely what the Islamic forms lacked, and Kuran builds the *Long Divergence* on dissolution-on-death. Asserting Islamic trajectory-binding in the temporal sense, in support of the coupling argument, would hand a referee the contradiction on the first page.
