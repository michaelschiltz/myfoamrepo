# The liability principle was demoted from rule to maxim

The liability principle is **not** distinctively Islamic, and framing it so invites correction from any Romanist. Roman and civilian law carry it in near-identical form: *ubi emolumentum, ibi onus*, and the rule of Paulus that the advantages of a thing naturally follow him whom its disadvantages follow (D. 50.17.10 — verify). The canonists held it too: *periculum sortis*, risk of the principal, stood among the extrinsic titles licensing gain, alongside *damnum emergens* and *lucrum cessans*. Return justified by risk borne is a shared Eurasian juridical commonplace.

The divergence is one of **status**, not possession. In fiqh, *al-ghunm bi-l-ghurm* remained an operative *qāʿida fiqhiyya* — a maxim with teeth, capable of invalidating a contract. In Europe the same principle survived but was demoted from constitutive rule to prudential counsel: the corporate form was built exempt from it, and the principle migrated out of law and into ethics.

Joaquín Setantí's aphorism is the fossil of that demotion. *"Sospechoso es el consejo del que induce y no peligra"* (*Centellas de varios conceptos*, 1614 [verify title and date]) is impeccable as moral admonition and wholly inert as law — written twelve years after the VOC charter, surviving in the courtly wisdom genre beside Gracián. **When a legal principle relocates into the aphorism, it has been demoted.** Compare the sea loan as the fossil *name* of a bundled instrument: same diagnostic, different object.

Guard against overdrawing this. Europe did not expel loss-sharing from law. General average — the *lex Rhodia de iactu* line, jettison losses shared proportionally across all interests — persisted continuously, as did mutual insurance and the unlimited-liability partnership. The corporation is a **carve-out** from the liability principle, not its abolition: an exemption subsequently extended until it became the default form of economic life.
