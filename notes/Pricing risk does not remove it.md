# Pricing risk does not remove it

Two inversions to refuse. First, on who bears the risk: the sea loan is a risk-*transfer from borrower to lender*. The borrower sheds sea-peril — that is the whole function of the non-recourse feature — and the lender *assumes* the risk the borrower offloaded, the elevated interest his compensation for assuming it. The lender is the risk-*absorber*, the proto-underwriter, not the risk-shedder.

Second, and decisive: **pricing risk does not remove it.** The premium raises the lender's *expected* return — his ensemble average across many imagined voyages. It does nothing whatever to his realized outcome when *this* ship goes down. On a single contract he is fully exposed to the tail. Pricing is ensemble-average compensation; it never touches the [[Time-average|time-average]] of the single trajectory. The war, blockade, or monsoon that takes the whole season's shipping at once is the time average reasserting itself and ruining the man who thought he had priced his risk away.

Diversifying across ventures reduces *idiosyncratic* risk only — a partial ergodicity restoration — but it *relocates* risk from idiosyncratic to systematic rather than removing it. The diversified lender has swapped exposure to any one wreck for exposure to the correlated tail that pooling cannot touch: the year the whole route closes. Diversification makes him a rentier, not safe.

Discipline for the prose: do not write "the price removed his risk" in your own voice. Attribute it to *him* — the ensemble-average illusion the project exists to puncture — then show the fleet going down together.
