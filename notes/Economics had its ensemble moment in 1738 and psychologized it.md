# Economics had its ensemble moment in 1738 and psychologized it

The claim that economics *never* produced a moment questioning the ensemble average is false and should be cut — it says the opposite of what is wanted. Economics produced that moment repeatedly and each time misresolved it. **Bernoulli (1738)** is exactly such a moment: the St. Petersburg payoff has infinite arithmetic expectation, nobody will pay much, so the expectation operator visibly fails. Bernoulli's response — log utility — is *formally a Kolmogorov–Nagumo quasi-arithmetic mean*, $f^{-1}(\mathbb{E}[f(x)])$: he kept the expectation and deformed the integrand.

That is the template economics has run ever since — expected utility, Allais, prospect theory — every "anomaly" resolved by bending the utility function or the probability weighting while the expectation operator stays sacrosanct. Peters' actual contribution is to show that for multiplicative dynamics the thing Bernoulli patched with psychology (log) falls straight out of the *time-average growth rate*, no preferences required.

So the honest historical claim is the sharper and more damning one: economics had the moment in 1738, psychologized it as risk aversion, and thereby buried a dynamical fact under a theory of mind for three centuries.
