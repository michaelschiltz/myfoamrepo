# Persona ficta made the corporation a-moral ab initio

Innocent IV held that the *universitas* is a *persona ficta*, that it has no soul, and that it therefore cannot be excommunicated and cannot itself commit a delict (Lyon, 1245 — verify the dating and exact formulation against Maitland or Gierke).

The corporation enters the world holding a certificate of moral immunity issued by the Church. Not immoral: **outside** the sacramental and moral order, because soulless. A-morality is therefore not a later degeneration of the form, nor a modern complaint retrofitted onto it, but a constitutive feature announced at its canonical birth.

The consequence is structural rather than rhetorical. Being a-moral by construction, the corporation contains no *internal* principle limiting to whom the tail may be relocated. Compare *al-ghunm bi-l-ghurm*, which travels with the instrument itself and voids any arrangement granting return without liability. The corporation's governors had therefore to be supplied externally and afterwards — regulation, tort, disclosure, fiduciary duty — and they have lagged the form ever since. **A-moral by construction, and therefore consequential.**
