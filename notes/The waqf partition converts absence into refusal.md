# The waqf partition converts absence into refusal

Islamic law possessed the technology of perpetuity. The *waqf* is an immortal endowment approaching legal personality, and Harris canvasses it in chapter 12 as the candidate from which a business corporation might have grown [verify]. Perpetuity was therefore *available*, and was withheld from profit-seeking enterprise.

That converts an absence into a **refusal**, and the distinction decides the argument. An absence is an incapacity and invites the deficit reading; a refusal is a doctrinal position and must be interpreted. The Islamic settlement is not a missing corporation but a *partition* — immortality granted to what serves the collective, denied to the private accumulation vehicle. Read alongside the circulation norm (Q 59:7, *kay lā yakūna dūlatan bayna al-aghniyāʾ minkum*, originally on the distribution of *fayʾ*), the *farāʾiḍ* fragmentation that Kuran scores as a brake looks less like archaic residue than like the operational arm of a norm against permanent, self-perpetuating reservoirs of wealth.

The comparative statement then tightens. Latin Christendom did not invent the perpetual person for trade either: the legal person is a canon-law construct built so the Church could hold property across the mortality of its clerics, migrating thence to municipalities, universities, and guilds. Both traditions built immortal entities; both reserved them first for sacred and collective ends. The divergence is not the presence of the technology but the **scope of its extension** — Christendom extended the immortal person to profit-seeking enterprise, Islamic law did not.

This forecloses the secularization thesis before a referee can raise it. The corporation is not a turn *away* from legal-theological doctrine; it is the secularization *of* a theological technology.
